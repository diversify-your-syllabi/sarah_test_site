Some Helpful Resources

Sarah Van Alsten
Last updated on Jan 23, 2020 2 min read

While there are not many readily available datasets that show citation counts for different authors by gender or sex, we can still direct you to a few places that focus on STEM diversity and education. Some data cleaning may be required :)!

STEM and Higher Education

First, the National Science Foundation has several datasets that may be useful, including the National Survey of College Graduates, the Survey of Doctoral Recipients, and the Survey of International Doctoral Recipients. These assess job prospects, salary, satisfaction, and career development in recipients of college degrees - both STEM and non-STEM. More generally, the National Center for Education Statistics collects data on high school and college enrollees, including other measures such as student loans, grades, and college majors.

Famous Female Statisticians

In honor of Women's History Month, the American Statistical Association posted a lovely collection of women currently working in math and statistics fields. You can click on any of their biographies to find out more about why they entered the field and what they're currently working on.

Pew Research Center

In one of their many opinion polls, the Pew Research Center interviewed men and women in STEM about perceived equity in the workplace. Generally, women viewed (in)equity as more of a problem than men, with almost 75% of those working in predominantly male spaces reporting harrassment, and 20% reporting that their gender made career advancement difficult. The data for the survey are here: note you will have to create a free account on the PEW website to download.

Data visualization

Data USA offers a deep dive into recipients of Biostatistics degrees: who gets them and from where, job prospects throughtout the country, and skills needed in a statistics career.
